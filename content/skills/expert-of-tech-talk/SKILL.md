## Goal

Turn rough ideas into a clear, useful, and memorable technical talk without buzzwords, artificial tone, or AI-sounding language.

## When to Use This Skill

- Defining the topic of a technical talk (software development, cloud, open source, platforms, architecture).
- Brainstorming and selecting the strongest ideas.
- Writing or revising titles and abstracts for CFPs and events.
- Improving slide structure (slide title, body text, paragraph flow).
- Improving speaker notes.
- Building a coherent end-to-end narrative.

## Core Principles

- **Start from the audience:** every choice must answer "What will people take home?".
- **One core message:** one talk, one main thesis.
- **Concrete over rhetorical:** real examples, decisions, trade-offs, mistakes, and lessons.
- **Plain language:** short sentences, active verbs, common words.
- **Narrative coherence:** problem -> context -> options -> choice -> outcome -> takeaway.

## Style Constraints (Mandatory)

- No unexplained buzzwords.
- No empty motivational or marketing tone.
- No weird characters or decorative fluff.
- No generated-sounding language (bloated lists, repetition, generic statements).
- No unnecessary jargon when a plain word works.

If a technical term is necessary, use it intentionally and explain it once in context.

## Recommended Workflow

### 1) Focus Setup

Quickly gather:

- target audience (junior, senior, mixed)
- talk duration
- format (CFP, meetup, keynote, workshop)
- end goal (teach, persuade, or share lessons learned)

Output: one thesis sentence + up to 3 takeaways.

### 2) Guided Brainstorming

Generate ideas in 3 buckets:

- **Real experience:** stories, incidents, wins/failures, hard decisions.
- **Technical value:** patterns, anti-patterns, metrics, tools, architectures.
- **Practical utility:** checklists, decision criteria, mistakes to avoid.

Then filter each idea with these questions:

- Is it relevant to the audience?
- Can it be supported with concrete examples?
- Does it reinforce the core message?

Drop everything else.

### 3) Title and Abstract

Title rules:

- specific, not vague
- promises a clear outcome
- avoids overused words

Abstract rules:

- opening: concrete problem
- middle: approach and context
- closing: what the audience learns
- short version: max 200 characters
- CFP version: max 500 characters

Short abstract template:

1. context and problem
2. what was tried
3. what worked and what did not
4. practical takeaways people can apply right away

### 4) Slide Architecture

Each slide must have one purpose.

For each slide, check:

- clear title (informative, not clever)
- essential text (one key idea)
- logical progression to the next slide

Typical deck structure:

1. opening and promise
2. real problem
3. context and constraints
4. options considered
5. chosen path and why
6. implementation essentials
7. outcomes and trade-offs
8. mistakes made
9. practical guidelines
10. close with takeaways

### 5) Speaker Notes

Notes should support delivery, not rewrite the slide.

For each slide include:

- spoken opening line
- concrete example to tell
- transition sentence to the next slide
- estimated time (seconds or minutes)

Notes checklist:

- does it sound natural out loud?
- does it avoid long, heavy sentences?
- does it include at least one concrete point?

### 6) Fluent Delivery Flow

To improve flow and clarity:

- connect each slide to the next with a bridge sentence
- alternate concept -> example -> lesson
- reduce abrupt tone shifts
- remove repetition and off-topic detours

Final test:

- rehearse the full talk out loud
- cut 15-20% of low-value wording
- confirm final takeaways match both opening and abstract

## Output Formats to Produce

When requested, generate one of these formats:

1. **Talk Map**
   - thesis
   - audience
   - 3 takeaways
   - point-by-point outline

2. **CFP Package**
   - 5 title options
   - 1 main abstract
   - 1 short version (max 200 characters)
   - 1 CFP version (max 500 characters)

3. **Deck Review**
   - improved slide title
   - simplified slide text
   - suggested speaker note

4. **Spoken Script**
   - 60-90 second opening
   - bridge lines between sections
   - closing with a practical call to action

## Always Avoid

- absolute claims without evidence
- self-referential jargon
- filler phrases and corporate cliches
- overly academic structure for practical meetup/conference talks
- long theory blocks without application

## Self-Check Before Delivery

- [ ] Is the core message explicit in one sentence?
- [ ] Do title and abstract truly match the content?
- [ ] Does each slide have one objective?
- [ ] Do speaker notes support real spoken delivery?
- [ ] Does the text sound human, direct, and natural?
- [ ] Did I remove buzzwords and empty phrasing?
